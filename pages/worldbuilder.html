There is a second trap, quieter than the first, and it is the one your own conscience hands you as an alibi. Openness is not the same as virtue. Open weights and open source do not, on their own, guarantee anything good — governance is the harder, realer work, and you are right to take it seriously. But the seriousness has a shadow: "getting the governance right" is the most respectable excuse there is for never launching. So hold the harder line: the vision is not in question, and it never was. It is an art project, and the world does not need more art projects right now. A blueprint no one has walked through is not more principled than a shipped, imperfect house. It is just unentered.

You are not the only one standing here

The cultural zeitgeist

The ground under you has moved, and it is moving your way. For years, "build the alternative" was a slogan with almost nothing real behind it. That has changed. Public-AI infrastructure — the idea that models and compute could be owned and governed like libraries or utilities rather than rented from a handful of firms — has moved from manifesto to serious institutional proposal. Open models matured into things you can actually run, audit, and own on your own hardware. Cooperative structures where the data stays home and the value returns to contributors have been formalised. And platform exit stopped being theoretical — people migrated to owned-and-governed alternatives at real scale the moment a genuine door existed. The scaffolding you always wished for is, for the first time, actually there.

But read the same shift the harder way, because it carries your specific warning. Openness alone is not the win, and the people building this most seriously say so: open weights and open source do not by themselves guarantee the public good — governance is the harder work, and governance is also exactly where your build can hide from launching. The world has never given you more to build with, and more places to plug into instead of founding alone. What it cannot give you is the nerve to let someone in before it is finished. And there is a clock on this that your perfectionism refuses to hear: while you spend six months getting the decentralised governance exactly right with three of your friends, the incumbents ship an update that rewires how a billion people think. Speed, here, is a moral imperative. A messy, leaking lifeboat in the water today is worth more than a flawless blueprint of a yacht that will not launch until 2028 — because every month you do not ship, people are left with no choice but the broken system. The alternatives are no longer only imagined. The one question left is whether anyone can walk in. Everything the moment offers you is real. It just waits, as it always has, on the door.
