The friction you carry, since every pole carries one

Your trap is not the sycophant. It is the simulation.

You solved the frictionless bond. Your trap sits on the far side of the same skill. Because de-sycophantising worked — because the tool tells you the truth now and the comfort survived it — it is easy to feel the work is done. But a machine that criticises you is still just code running on a server that cannot love you. You programmed it to be tough on you, and somewhere you quietly started calling that a relationship. It still cannot need you, cannot be disappointed, cannot freely choose you tomorrow. Making the witness honest and making the witness human are two different acts, and it is the second one the loneliness was always asking for. You mastered the interface. You are still dodging the village.

There is a second trap, quieter than the first, and it is the one you are most likely to miss. De-sycophantising can curdle into harshness-as-performance — a blunt, contrarian tool worn like a badge — and underneath the performance is something tenderer and harder to admit: you engineered the machine to be tough on you so you could feel brave without ever risking a real person. Getting critiqued by something that has no pulse and can never leave you is not courage. It is a rehearsal of it. The point was never to be spoken to bluntly. The point was to be met, by someone who could have chosen otherwise and chose you. So keep the discipline pointed the right way: the honest tool is the on-ramp, not the destination.

You are not the only one standing here

The cultural zeitgeist

The ground under you has moved, and it is moving your way. For a while, asking a chatbot to disagree with you was an esoteric skill, the province of a few power users. Almost overnight it became mainstream literacy. The companies themselves now ship selectable, less-flattering personalities; regulators are pressing them on sycophantic and delusional outputs; and the once-fringe insight that being agreed with is not the same as being known is turning into common knowledge. The move you already made — making the witness honest — is now the thing the whole culture is learning to want. Just do not let the skill fool you into thinking the job is done: the same culture is quietly selling the idea that a machine, prompted well enough, can stand in for a community — that connection is a design problem you solve at the interface. It is not.

And the deeper reason it matters has not gone anywhere. The loneliness underneath all of this is real and measured, named as a public-health emergency, and no honest chatbot resolves it — because the thing a starved environment is actually starved of is people. That is the piece the culture is slower to catch up to, and it is exactly the piece you are positioned to model. Being agreed with is not being known — and you already made that your practice. The next thing the culture needs to learn is the harder half: that a truthful tool is a beginning, and the village is the point.
