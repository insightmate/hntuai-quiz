The mirror

What you were doing long before the tool

None of this started with AI. You were using taste to keep yourself safe long before the machine arrived — leaving the room when the vibe was off, dismissing a trend before you'd tried it, reading someone's taste as a proxy for whether they could be trusted. The discernment kept you out of places that might make you feel ordinary, replaceable, or misread. AI did not invent the vigilance. It handed the vigilance a visible enemy. Mark the ones you recognise.

  • You spot an em-dash or a too-smooth sentence and your brain says AI before you've finished reading.
  • You have run a passage through a detector and treated the percentage as a verdict.
  • You have come close to accusing a friend's heartfelt message of being written by a machine.
  • You are loudly anti-AI in public, and you quietly use it for small things in private.
  • You spend more energy auditing whether other people's work is real than making your own.

A true result comes with a little discomfort. If this had felt like a compliment, it would have missed you.

The catalyst

When the reflex fires hardest

The suspicion does not fire evenly. There are specific moments the verdict lands before you have decided anything. Watch for these four. One of them is yours.

  1. 01The em-dash. It lands mid-paragraph and the word AI arrives before you have finished the line.
  2. 02The too-smooth passage. A tonal cleanness with no snags, and the clench of recognition-and-disgust.
  3. 03The friend's polished text. Something warm and well-written, and the small ugly thought: did they even write this?
  4. 04The detector percentage. A number on a screen, and the relief of a verdict that lets you stop feeling and start being right.
The tell is a tightening — a clench in the jaw and shoulders, a spike of recognition-and-disgust the instant a tell appears. The body braces before the mind has finished reading. Learn that clench. It is fear, arriving disguised as judgement.
The cost, and it is being paid now

The border keeps moving, so you keep re-drawing it.

Here is the trap in one sentence. The tells are a moving target and the eye is shakier than its owner believes. The em-dash you are hunting is a mark humans have used for centuries. The tricolon is rhythm. The smoothness is often just a careful writer, or a non-native one, or a neurodivergent one whose style is patterned and explicit. Every time you resolve the ambiguity into a rule, the rule catches a human. The border does not hold still, so the re-drawing of it has quietly become the work.

The message you almost accused

A friend sends you something they clearly meant — a long, warm note, an em-dash right in the middle of it. You feel the clench before you feel the warmth. You read it a second time, hunting. Your thumb hovers over a reply that says did you use AI for this? And in that second you are no longer protecting anything human. You are prosecuting the exact irregularity where the human lives — and worse, you are doing the machine's job for it, flattening the quirk the model could never have made. Every time you police a human quirk, you are doing the machine's job for it. Read that twice. Every time you police a human quirk, you are doing the machine's job for it.

And the evidence is blunt now, in a way it was not a year ago. The detectors you half-trust misclassify human writing at meaningful rates — commonly in the double digits, and far higher for people writing in a second language, where false-positive rates have been reported past sixty per cent. A student was expelled after a tool flagged her essay as almost entirely machine-written. She had written every word. The eye that meant to protect human work is being used to convict it.

And here is the part the principle keeps hidden. It is so much easier to take apart someone else's paragraph than to write your own. Your trap is not the machine. It is your own cynicism — critique became the safest room in the house, and somewhere in there your own output thinned, because nothing you make could survive the purity test you hold everyone else to. A guardian who only knows how to lock the gate eventually forgets how to build the house. The vigilance is not integrity. It is fear that learned to sound like taste.

About the certainty you cannot quite verify

There is probably a quiet unease underneath the confidence — a sense that you have been sure, out loud, more times than you have actually checked. Do not turn it into shame. Your eye is real, and the discomfort that made you distrust the slop was accurate before anyone else's. The trouble is not the eye. It is the certainty the eye got fused to. A scared guardian and a good one look identical right up until the moment one of them is wrong about a person.

The stack of good reasons

A true argument can still be doing a defensive job

Tell-hunting is only the visible layer. Underneath it, the same instinct reaches for bigger reasons — the ecological cost of the machine, the theft the models were trained on, the way it hollows out your own capacity to think, the threat to human art. Here is the part that is easy to miss, so let it land slowly: those arguments are often true, and important, and worth making. The ecological cost is real. The training-data theft is real. None of it is being waved away here.

But a true argument can still be doing a second, quieter job. It can be the coat the fear puts on, so that refusal gets to feel like principle. There is a way to tell the difference, and it is a single question: would you still refuse if the reason were resolved? If the machine ran on clean power and paid every artist tomorrow, and the wall stayed up anyway — then the environment was never the root. The fear was. The reasons are just the most defensible thing to point at when the actual feeling — what if my eye is obsolete, what if my taste is worth nothing now — is the one thing you cannot say out loud.

This is not a nudge to drop the arguments. Keep the ones that are true; the world needs them made well. It is a nudge to know which weight each one is actually carrying, so that your refusal, where you choose it, is genuinely chosen — and not the fear wearing whichever principle the room will most respect.

You are not the only one standing here

The cultural zeitgeist

You are standing inside a genuine cultural episode, and it is worth naming so you can tell your own eye apart from the panic around it. The disgust you run on got vindicated: "slop" was named a word of the year, a human-made counter-market began to form, and publishers started stamping books as human-written. You were right about something real, and the culture caught up to you. But the same two years handed you an indictment alongside the vindication. The mark of the human became the thing under suspicion — an actual movement sprang up to defend the em-dash from the accusation of being a machine tell.

And the tools of detection were quietly discredited even as they spread. Independent testing found detectors misclassifying human writing at rates from the low double digits to well over half for second-language writers, while a real student was expelled on a near-total false flag she did not deserve. Meanwhile the very definition of the border you want to defend is dissolving — experts now say that with the machine so pervasive, defining what counts as "AI-free" has become genuinely hard, and consumers cannot agree on what "human-made" even means. Your eye was right about the slop. The tools you reach for to prove it are wrong about people. The border you keep re-drawing cannot be secured by inspection, because inspection is exactly what the moment has made unreliable. Seeing that clearly is the first thing you can stop policing.
