The mirror

The decision you keep making without announcing it

This is not about talent. It is about a small, repeated choice: you put the risk back in. You let the machine hold the boring parts and you keep the sentences that carry you. Mark the ones you recognise.

  • You reject a suggestion because it "isn't how I'd say it," even when the cleaner version is right there.
  • You leave a misspelling or an odd phrasing in on purpose, because it sounds like you actually talking.
  • You caught yourself avoiding your own favourite phrasing in case it "sounds like AI." And you kept it anyway.
  • At 11pm you argue with the model over one paragraph, throw out three suggestions, and keep the fourth because it genuinely sharpened what you meant.
  • The condolence card, the eulogy, the message that matters. You write those by hand. The risk of the wrong word is the proof of the care.

Recognition without a victory lap is the signal. If this felt like applause, it missed the discipline underneath it.

Where it actually gets hard

When the line goes blurry

Your fight is not with disappearing. You already won that one. It is with a subtler moment, the one where you cannot tell whether an edit is clarifying your voice or quietly sanding it off. Watch for these four.

  1. 01The clean edit that would genuinely help. It improves the line. You cannot yet tell if taking it sharpens you or erases you.
  2. 02The tired afternoon. The model's fluency starts to feel like your own competence, and the smooth version slips through.
  3. 03The room that reads you as a machine. Someone calls your natural cadence "AI," and you feel the pull to prove you are human by writing blander.
  4. 04The compliment you cannot take in. Someone tells you they loved the piece, and it will not land, because you cannot yet tell whether they loved you or the machine's fluency wearing your name.
The tell lives in the half-second before you accept a suggestion. A small hesitation in the body. Learn that pause. It is your discernment asking the only question that matters here: does this edit clarify me, or replace me? The whole craft is learning to answer it fast.
The friction you carry, since every pole carries one

Your trap is not hiding. It is guarding.

You solved the disappearing. Your trap sits on the far side of the same instinct. You guard the voice so hard that you turn down the edit that would have made it land, and over-protection wears the exact face of integrity, which is what makes it so hard to catch. A voice defended into a fortress stops reaching anyone.

Eleven o'clock, the fourth rejected edit

You have turned down four suggestions on one paragraph. Three of them deserved it. The fourth would have made the sentence clearer, and you killed it anyway, because accepting the help felt like losing the voice. The reader would have understood you better. You protected the paragraph from them.

There is a second trap, quieter than the first. The machine's fluency feels like competence, and on a tired day you take the smooth line and call it finished. And there is a cost that is not yours to fix at all. A culture now hunting for machine "tells" will sometimes point at you, because the very things it calls artificial, the long unfurling sentence, the odd punctuation, the formal register, are for many writers the fossil record of their own education, not a machine's fingerprint. The quirk that proves you were there is the thing you get accused for.

You are not the only one standing here

The cultural zeitgeist

The ground under you is moving, and it is worth reading properly, because it is moving your way. "Slop" became a word of the year. A human-made counter-market is forming — publishers have started stamping books "human written," and readers are learning to pay for a pulse they can trust. The voiceless work that floods every inbox got a name and a price tag, and the people who receive it trust the sender less. For years, keeping your voice was effortful and unrewarded. The market has only just started to reward it.

And yet the same culture that has started to prize human irregularity is teaching humans to sand it off. People hunt for "tells" — the em dash, the long unfurling sentence, the formal register — and point them at humans, and writers began filing their own fingerprints down to dodge the accusation. You may have felt the pull yourself. The irony is total, because the marks being called artificial are, for a great many of us, the fossil record of an education, the exact grain of a real voice. The thing they are calling a machine's fingerprint is often the proof a human was there. Underneath the panic, the shift keeps running your way. As the slop rises, the unmistakable voice becomes the thing that cannot be copied. You are early, not wrong.
