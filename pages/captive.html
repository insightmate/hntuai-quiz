The mirror

What you were doing long before the mandate

None of this started with AI. You were already overriding the body to stay acceptable — staying in the job, the routine, the room that made you go numb, because the cost of leaving felt too high, and saying yes while explaining why the yes was reasonable. The corporate mandate did not create the pattern. It placed it inside a survival narrative. Mark the ones you recognise.

  • When a task gets hard, you reach for a digital shortcut to numb the friction before you have even felt it.
  • You do the thing and, in the same breath, explain why the thing is fine. "It's not ideal, but at this point I just…"
  • You have gotten worse at a skill you used to be good at since letting AI do it, and only noticed after the fact.
  • You cannot sit and do nothing without reaching for a screen to fill the silence.
  • You collapse into a screen and call it rest, then wonder why the tiredness never lifts.

A true result comes with a little discomfort. If this had felt like a compliment, it would have missed you.

The catalyst

When the override fires hardest

You do not swallow the ick evenly. There are specific moments the hand moves on its own, before you have decided anything. Watch for these four. One of them is yours.

  1. 01The hard task. You reach for the shortcut to numb the friction before you have registered it, so you never have to feel the difficulty.
  2. 02The mandate. The dashboard, the review, the "AI is no longer optional" email. You comply, and you apologise for the tool in the same breath.

The cost, and it is being paid now

The race has no finish line. That's the design.

Here is the part they do not put on the dashboard. You are inside a real race, with real economic teeth, and you cannot win it — not because you are not fast enough, but because it was never built to be won. Becoming a better cog is not the exit. It is the deepest part of the trap. You cannot out-hustle a machine, and every hour spent trying to is an hour the machine gets to keep.

Eleven o'clock, the phone within reach

Racing brain, numb body. You pick up the book and read the same paragraph three times. The phone is right there, so you reach for it — just one more scroll — and the reaching is so automatic you barely feel it happen. In the morning the dread arrives before the alarm does. Your body has been sounding this alarm at full volume for months. You have been turning the volume down.

And there is a second cost, quieter and more permanent. Every task you hand over is a skill you stop rehearsing. It does not announce itself. You just get slowly worse at the thing, and only notice after it has cost you something. The clinical data is blunt about this: when the assistance is taken away, the unassisted skill has measurably dropped. The saved hour, meanwhile, already got spent. The only question is who spent it, and on what.

About every yes you have swallowed

There is probably a low, familiar guilt about all the times you felt the no and stayed anyway. Do not turn it on yourself. The compliance was rational. You were responding to a real threat, inside a system built by people who see you as a resource to optimise, and "just unplug" is useless advice when a mortgage is attached to the mandate. The exposure here is of the situation, not of a weakness in you. You are not the broken part. The room is.

The other end of your axis

The Cultivator

Your axis has a far end, and the Cultivator is standing on it. Same compass. Same ick, felt just as sharply. The move is not to quit everything tomorrow. That is not available to most people, and pretending otherwise is its own kind of cruelty. The move is smaller and more within reach: to stop overriding the reading, one ick at a time, and to build one real thing the algorithm cannot touch.

For later. Think of the last time the fantasy of making something with your hands arrived, and then closed the second you opened the laptop. That fantasy is not escapism. It is the compass pointing at the door.

You are not the only one standing here

The cultural zeitgeist

You are standing at a real inflection point, and it is worth naming so you stop taking the pressure as a personal failing. This is the moment AI use moved from encouraged to enforced. Major employers now tell managers that using AI is "no longer optional" and weigh it in performance reviews; some run live dashboards of how much each person uses it, feeding promotion decisions. Roughly three quarters of knowledge workers use it, most feel pressure to adopt before they are ready, and around two in five now fear being made obsolete. The message from every direction is the same: adopt or be left behind.

What almost no one is telling you, inside that noise, is that the race is engineered. It has no finish line by design, and the "freedom" the agentic hype keeps promising is partly vapour — a large share of those projects are forecast to be cancelled, even as the dependency they build is real. The counter-evidence for another way of living exists. It has just not reached you yet, because the enclosure is loud and the door is quiet. The dread is not you failing to keep up. It is you, keeping perfect count. Read that twice. You are keeping perfect count. Your body did the maths the dashboard cannot show, and this whole page is permission to read the total.
