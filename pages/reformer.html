The catalyst

The loop does not pull evenly. There are specific moments the outrage restocks and the objection writes itself before you have decided anything. Watch for these four. One of them is yours.

  1. 01The fresh scandal. A new harm, a new extraction, and the shelf of things to be right about is restocked for another week.
  2. 02The meeting. You name it precisely, the room agrees, nothing moves — and "at least I said it" does the work that change was supposed to.
  3. 03The eleven o'clock thread. One more accurate reply, the old cocktail of vindication and helplessness, and no object at the end of it.
  4. 04The agreeable model. The tool concurs that the system is broken, validates the critique perfectly, and never once nudges you toward the build.

The cost, and it is being paid now

The house metabolises your critique.

Here is the trap in one line. A system can survive being accurately described. It cannot survive being replaced — and you are spending all your energy on the first. The institutions you critique are not threatened by your critique; they are often comfortable with it. There is even a name now for the way the ethical debate is kept running precisely so it never has to be acted on, a performance staged to reassure and to delay real rules. In-house ethicists are hired and then kept far from the design, because a critic on the payroll is proof the company is "listening." Responsible-AI teams are stood up and quietly cut. The debate is funded so the change need not be. Say the uncomfortable part plainly: you are not the resistance. You are the pressure-relief valve. As long as you are busy writing the perfect objection, you are not building the competitor — and a devastating critique that changes nothing is, in the end, just a diary entry the house is glad you keep.
